EU Initiates Anti-Dumping Investigation on Chinese Fasteners

Historic Case Threatens Over 1,700 Chinese Exporters with Nearly $800 Million at Stake

首页 / 新闻 / EU Initiates Anti-Dumping Investigation on Chinese Fasteners
发布于 2017年11月9日

On November 9, 2007, the European Commission officially announced the initiation of an anti-dumping proceeding concerning imports of certain iron or steel fasteners originating in the People's Republic of China[citation:5]. The announcement was published in the Official Journal of the European Union on the same day, opening what would become the largest anti-dumping case ever faced by Chinese exporters at that time[citation:1][citation:3].

Background and Filing of the Complaint

The investigation was initiated following a complaint lodged by the European Industrial Fasteners Institute (EIFI), an organization representing more than 25% of the total Community production of certain iron and steel fasteners[citation:5]. EIFI alleged that imports of the concerned products from China were being dumped, thereby causing material injury to the Community industry.

  • Product coverage Wood screws (excluding coach screws), self-tapping screws, other screws and bolts with heads, and washers, falling under CN codes 7318 12 90, 7318 14 91, 7318 14 99, 7318 15 59, 7318 15 69, 7318 15 81, 7318 15 89, 7318 15 90, 7318 21 00, and 7318 22 00[citation:5].
  • Investigation period October 1, 2006 to September 30, 2007[citation:4].
  • Response deadline Exporters had 15 days from November 9 to submit sampling information and market economy status claims[citation:5].
  • Potential duties If dumping was confirmed, duties could reach up to 87%[citation:8].

The case was unprecedented in scale. According to Chinese customs data, exports of steel fasteners from China to the EU during the investigation period reached approximately US$809 million (€575 million), involving more than 1,700 enterprises across China[citation:1][citation:2][citation:8]. The city of Ningbo, a major fastener manufacturing hub, accounted for nearly US$100 million of these exports and involved over 100 local companies[citation:3][citation:9].

Impact on Chinese Exporters

China was and remains the world's largest producer of fasteners. At the time of the investigation, China had over 1,000 fastener manufacturing enterprises, with Ningbo alone accounting for more than 25% of national sales. The industry generated annual revenue exceeding RMB 10 billion, with over 20 companies posting annual sales above RMB 100 million[citation:3][citation:9].

Immediate consequences for exporters: The threat of anti-dumping duties ranging from 63% to 87% loomed over Chinese manufacturers[citation:8]. Many feared that a negative ruling would completely exclude them from the EU market, which was China's second-largest fastener market after North America[citation:9].

Previous trade actions: This was not the first such action against Chinese fasteners. In 2004, the EU had already imposed anti-dumping duties of 11.4% to 27.4% on stainless steel fasteners from China[citation:3][citation:5]. Canada had also launched combined anti-dumping and countervailing duty investigations that forced Chinese exporters to withdraw from that market entirely[citation:9].

Chinese Response and Defense Strategy

In response to the investigation, the China Chamber of Commerce for Import and Export of Machinery and Electronic Products (CCCME) convened an emergency coordination meeting in Ningbo to organize industry-wide defense efforts[citation:3][citation:9]. Chinese companies pursued two primary defense strategies:

  • Collective industry defense Participating in a joint "no injury" defense to demonstrate that Chinese imports had not harmed EU producers.
  • Individual responses Some companies, including Ningbo Yonghong Fastener Manufacturing and Ningbo Jingding Fastener, hired their own legal counsel to seek individual treatment and market economy status[citation:1][citation:9].

Eight companies had committed to responding to the investigation with their own funding by early November 2007[citation:9]. The Ningbo Fastener Industry Association called on enterprises to strengthen self-discipline and avoid cutthroat price competition that could fuel dumping allegations[citation:9].

Chinese exporters strongly disputed the allegations. The Jiaxing Association of Fastener Import and Export Companies pointed out that according to the European Commission's own analysis, the profitability of European manufacturers increased 110% between 2003 and 2007, with similar improvements in production levels, sales volumes, and employment, which grew by 12%[citation:8]. "No matter how hard we look at the European Commission's analysis, we cannot see how Chinese exports of fasteners have hurt European manufacturers in any way," said Zhang Feng, deputy secretary of the association[citation:8].

Controversies and Unfair Treatment

Two European-owned Chinese subsidiaries received special exemptions. The two companies—Agrati's Chinese unit and Celo's Chinese unit—were granted individual examination and ultimately exempted from duties, while all other Chinese exporters faced prohibitive tariffs[citation:8]. The Chinese industry questioned the fairness of European manufacturers requesting protection against Chinese fasteners while continuing to import from their own Chinese subsidiaries duty-free[citation:8].

The European Commission selected India as the analogue country for determining normal value, a choice that Chinese exporters argued was inappropriate given significant differences in production costs and market conditions between China and India[citation:5].

WTO rules were allegedly violated during the investigation. China later argued that the EU failed to comply with WTO provisions regarding dumping margin calculations, injury determination, causation analysis, and information disclosure[citation:2][citation:4].

Timeline and Ultimate Resolution

  • November 2007 Investigation initiated[citation:4][citation:5].
  • January 2009 Definitive anti-dumping duties imposed for five years—77.5% weighted average for responding companies and 85% for non-responding companies[citation:1][citation:4].
  • July 31, 2009 China filed its first-ever WTO dispute complaint against the EU (DS397)[citation:2][citation:4].
  • July 2011 WTO Appellate Body ruled in China's favor, finding numerous procedural and substantive WTO violations by the EU[citation:10].
  • February 2016 EU formally repealed anti-dumping duties on Chinese fasteners, nearly seven years after their imposition[citation:10].

This case became a landmark in China's use of the WTO dispute settlement mechanism. It marked the first time China challenged an EU anti-dumping measure at the WTO and ultimately prevailed, establishing important precedents regarding non-market economy treatment and the obligations of investigating authorities[citation:4][citation:10].

However, the damage had already been done. The Chinese fastener industry suffered severe losses during the years the duties were in place, with many smaller exporters forced out of the EU market entirely. Even after the duties were lifted in 2016, the industry had lost significant market share and faced an uphill battle to recover lost ground[citation:1][citation:10].

Lessons for Today's Exporters

The 2007-2016 EU fastener case offers enduring lessons for Chinese exporters facing trade remedy investigations. First, early and aggressive defense—including both collective industry action and individual legal representation—can improve outcomes. Second, utilizing WTO dispute settlement mechanisms, while lengthy, can ultimately overturn unfair measures. Third, market diversification remains essential to mitigate the impact of trade barriers in any single region.
