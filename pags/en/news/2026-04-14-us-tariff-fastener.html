U.S. Tariff Policy Adjustment: Fastener Exports Face a New Turning Point

Section 232 Tariff Rules Shift: Tax Base Changes from "Metal Content" to "Total Product Value"—How Can Chinese Fastener Enterprises Navigate Rising Export Costs?

Home / News / U.S. Tariff Policy Adjustment: Fastener Exports Face a New Turning Point
Published on April 14, 2026

On April 6, 2026, the latest implementation rules for Section 232 tariffs on steel and aluminum derivative products, jointly issued by the U.S. Department of Commerce and U.S. Customs and Border Protection (CBP), officially took effect. This adjustment covers a wide range of goods, and fasteners—as typical products containing steel and aluminum—have become one of the most directly affected categories. For Chinese fastener manufacturers and exporters deeply engaged in the U.S. market, this policy shift signals a fundamental restructuring of export cost dynamics.

Core Change: Tax Base Shifts from "Metal Component" to "Total Product Value"

Previously, the calculation of Section 232 tariffs on steel and aluminum derivative products was relatively complex—duties were only applied to the steel or aluminum raw material portion contained within the product, meaning the tax base was the "value of the metal content." However, the new regulation explicitly states: Effective April 6, 2026, all derivative products classified under HTSUS Chapter 73 (Iron/Steel Articles), Chapter 74 (Copper Articles), and Chapter 76 (Aluminum Articles) will uniformly be subject to a 25% additional tariff based on the **total declared value of the entire product**.

This change has a profound impact on the fastener industry. Consider a typical high-strength bolt: The total product value includes raw material costs, cold heading processing fees, heat treatment costs, surface finishing expenses, and packaging and freight. Under the old rules, only 30%-50% of the product value (the metal content portion) was subject to the 25% tariff. Under the new rules, the **entire 100% value** of the bolt is taxed at 25%. This significantly expands the tax base and directly compresses profit margins for exporting companies.

  • Old Rule Scenario: Product value $1.00, Metal content $0.40 → Tariff base $0.40 → 25% Duty = $0.10 additional cost.
  • New Rule Scenario: Product value $1.00 → Tariff base $1.00 → 25% Duty = $0.25 additional cost.
  • Cost Increase Ratio: The actual tariff burden increases by approximately 150%.
  • Affected HS Codes: 7318 (Screws, Bolts, Nuts), 7317 (Nails, Tacks), 7616 (Aluminum Fasteners), etc.

For export enterprises that rely on "volume-driven, thin-margin" models, the profitability of orders under existing long-term contracts will face severe challenges. Especially for customized non-standard fasteners with high processing costs and high added value, the tax burden increase is even more pronounced.

Industry Impact: Export Momentum Under Pressure in the Short Term

According to data from the General Administration of Customs of China, the United States remains one of the top destinations for Chinese fastener exports. While market diversification has reduced dependency on the U.S. market in recent years, the U.S. still accounts for a significant share—approximately 12%-15%—of total fastener exports. This policy adjustment may trigger three levels of chain reactions in the short term:

1. Order Price Renegotiation and Transfer Pressure The sudden nature of the policy has forced Chinese exporters to immediately engage in urgent consultations with U.S. importers. Typically, U.S. buyers are reluctant to fully absorb the 25% tariff increase, while Chinese suppliers lack the profit margin to absorb it entirely. This may lead to a wave of order cancellations or shipment delays.

2. Supply Chain Relocation Acceleration To circumvent the high tariffs on finished fasteners, some multinational end-users may expedite the relocation of fastener sourcing to countries not covered by Section 232 tariffs, such as Mexico, Canada, or Southeast Asia. This poses a long-term challenge to China's position as a global fastener manufacturing hub.

Corporate Response Strategies: Four Dimensions for Navigating the Cycle

In response to this sudden trade policy shift, Chinese fastener companies need to adopt systematic countermeasures. WT Fasteners, based on its deep industry experience and supply chain management capabilities, recommends companies focus on the following four areas:

  • 1. Precision Cost Accounting and Pricing Model Adjustment Immediately update the ERP system's tariff calculation logic and accurately reflect the 25% comprehensive tariff in new quotes to avoid "negative margin orders."
  • 2. Accelerate Market Diversification Reduce over-reliance on a single U.S. market. Actively expand into RCEP member countries (Japan, South Korea, Australia), the EU, and the domestic "domestic substitution" high-end market.
  • 3. Explore Overseas Warehouse Models and Processing Trade Consider establishing finished goods inventory in bonded zones or transit hubs (e.g., Mexico) to defer tariff payments or change the country-of-origin compliance status.
  • 4. Enhance Product Added Value and Technology Barriers Shift from selling standard parts to providing "Fastener Solutions." By strengthening R&D in high-strength alloys and surface treatment technologies, reduce price sensitivity among customers and mitigate the impact of tariffs.

Conclusion and Outlook

Tariffs as a Catalyst for Industry Advancement Every adjustment in international trade rules serves as a stress test for the global competitiveness of China's manufacturing sector. The expansion of U.S. Section 232 tariffs has undoubtedly increased the cost and complexity of exporting fasteners in the short term. However, from a long-term perspective, this also compels Chinese fastener enterprises to accelerate their transition from being "cost-driven" to being "technology and brand-driven."
